Virtual California Earthquake Simulator

Virtual California (VC) is a boundary element code that models the earthquake fault system in California. It uses geologic observations to define fault topology, slip rates, and frictional parameters. The faults are meshed into fault segments, the functional elements of the simulation. Virtual California is designed to explore the statistics of seismicity in today's faults by simulating stress interactions between individual fault elements. Earthquakes are triggered by elements reaching their stress threshold values, which are determined by the fault model. During the rupture (earthquake) elements fail (slip) and release stress.

Computing Earthquake Probabilities

The simulation output relevant to computing earthquake probabilities are the recurrance times, or the time between successive earthquakes on a given fault. We can take these recurrance times from the simulation output, and by computing the cumulative histogram of recurrence times we compute the cumulative probability distribution for an earthquake on the specified fault. From this distribution we can also compute the average waiting times until the next major earthquake, as a function of the time since the last large earthquake. Surface Displacements

InSAR interferogram showing vertical displacements for a large earthquake simulated earthquake (similar magnitude, rupture length and average slip as the 1906 San Francisco earthquake) on the northern San Andreas Fault, as seen by an orbiting satellite. Vertical displacements are computed via Okada's half-space deformation functions.

Surface Gravity Changes

VC computes gravity changes for the same simulated event by passing the fault geometry and the slips for each element as input into Okubo's gravity Green's functions. These statistical data sets of gravity changes and vertical displacements can help develop and test future satellite-based earthquake/tsunami early warning systems.

Fundamental Gravity Patterns

Co-seismic Gravity Changes

This video is a way of "watching" 100 years of a Virtual California simulation. This is not a time dependent evolution of the gravity field, but is a visualization of co-seismic gravity changes for all earthquakes in the time frame. Gravity fields fade with time to smooth the animation. The majority of the events are strike-slip faults (plate boundary similar to rubbing two bricks together on their long edge) and have a characteristic butterfly pattern. The inset plot on the lower left is the cumulative frequency-magnitude distribution.

Frequency vs. Magnitude

Earthquake frequency simulated by VC, averaged over 500,000 years of simulated time, plotted as function of magnitude, red error bars denote the observed earthquake rates in California, and the gray line is the Gutenberg-Richter law. Deficiency of simulated events below magnitude 6.0 is due to the finite size of each fault element in the simulation; smaller fault elements would yield more small events.

Magnitude plotted as function of the log of rupture area, red line is the published scaling relationship by Wells and Coppersmith.
